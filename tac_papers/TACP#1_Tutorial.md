## 1. Why Do Farts Make Us Laugh?
A fart is a universal bodily phenomenon—
a physiological expulsion accompanied by sound and odor.
This study does not address its biological mechanisms.
Instead, it focuses on the cognitive, bodily, and cultural structures
underlying a widely observed phenomenon:

**“Why do children laugh at farts?”**

Across early childhood, laughter in response to farting occurs with striking frequency.
Yet sneezing—another sudden, uncontrollable sound—rarely elicits the same laughter.
This discrepancy is empirically obvious,
but existing research has not adequately explained it.

Traditional explanations rely on simple stimulus–response models:
“the sound is sudden,”
“the rhythm is irregular,”
and so on.
However, such frameworks oversimplify children’s cognition
and fail to account for several crucial elements:

- the perception of odor and accumulated experiences of disgust

- the bodily experience of “something coming out of me”

- the internalization of cultural taboos surrounding farting

- the conceptual depth formed by integrating these elements

This study adopts the position that laughter emerges
not from any single factor,
but from the interaction of these components.
Specifically, it proposes a multilayered model in which:

immediacy of sound,
memory of odor,
bodily experience,
and cultural meaning

overlap and reinforce one another.

Furthermore, the study formalizes the emergence of laughter
as a probabilistic model consisting of two layers:

the immediate stimulus (S)  
and
the maturity of the underlying concept (C).

This framework allows for a quantitative explanation
of why “farts” and “sneezes,”
despite sharing properties such as being involuntary, audible,
and physiologically natural,
produce markedly different laughter responses in children.

The study also examines the phenomenon in which farting often involves
a value shift—from disgust (negative) to laughter (positive)—
and analyzes the structural background of this transformation.

The goal of this research is to present
an integrated and formal account
of the cognitive, bodily, and cultural structures
that enable children’s laughter toward farting.

## 2. Limitations of Existing Research
This chapter outlines the theoretical limitations in prior studies that attempt to explain children’s laughter toward farting. Existing frameworks rely primarily on stimulus–response explanations and fail to adequately address key factors such as odor, bodily experience, concept formation, and cultural taboos.

---

### 2.1 Limitations of Stimulus–Response Models
Traditional explanations emphasize properties of the sound stimulus:

- its suddenness

- its irregular rhythm

- its unpredictability

These accounts treat laughter as a direct reaction to auditory features.

However, this framework reduces children’s cognition to overly simple stimulus–response mechanisms.
It cannot explain why sneezing—also a sudden, uncontrollable sound—rarely elicits laughter.
Sound alone is insufficient to capture the observed differences between the two phenomena.

---

### 2.2 Neglect of Odor as a Contributing Factor
Farts involve not only sound but also odor.
As children grow, they develop the concept of “fart = smell” through:

- odor perception

- accumulated experiences of disgust

- associations with excretion

Despite this, odor is rarely treated as a condition for laughter in existing research.
This omission overlooks the multisensory nature of fart-related experiences and represents a significant theoretical gap.

---

### 2.3 Lack of Attention to Concept Formation
Children construct the concept of “fart” through the integration of multiple experiences:

- sound

- smell

- bodily sensations

- social reactions (adults’ laughter, scolding, teasing)

The maturity of this conceptual network directly influences both the intensity and probability of laughter.
Yet prior research has largely ignored concept formation as a dimension of cognitive development.

---

### 2.4 Insufficient Connection to Cultural Taboos
In many cultures, farting is treated as “unclean.”
Children learn this taboo through adult reactions and social norms.

This cultural meaning strongly shapes:

- whether farting is positioned as something one may laugh at

- how intense the laughter becomes

However, within developmental psychology, the internalization of cultural taboos and its influence on laughter has received little attention.

---

## 3. Central Claim of This Study: The Four‑Layer Structural Model
This study argues that children’s laughter toward farting arises from the interaction of four layers:

immediacy of sound,
memory of odor,
bodily experience,
and cultural meaning.

Whereas prior research has focused disproportionately on auditory stimuli,
the present model emphasizes the process through which farting becomes a multisensory, embodied, and culturally “deep” concept.

---

### 3.1 Layer One: Immediacy of Sound (Trigger)
The sound of a fart is characterized by:

- suddenness

- irregularity

- variability (pitch, duration, explosive vs. frictional sounds)

These features function as an immediate trigger, perceived as an “unpredictable anomalous sound.”

However, sound alone does not produce laughter.
The fact that sneezes or hiccups—also sudden, uncontrollable sounds—rarely elicit comparable laughter demonstrates that auditory stimuli are insufficient as a sole explanation.

---

### 3.2 Layer Two: Delayed Odor (Conceptual Core)
Farts involve odor that arrives with a delay.
This delay contributes to children’s experiences of:

- perceiving disgust

- associating the smell with excretion

- conceptualizing farting as “something that smells”

Through development, children form the concept “fart = something smelly,”  
which becomes the semantic foundation for laughter.

Sound alone is merely an anomalous noise;
odor memory transforms it into a distinct conceptual category: “a fart.”

---

### 3.3 Layer Three: Integration of Bodily Experience (Embodied Cognition)
A fart is something that comes from one’s own body, accompanied by:

- uncontrollable bodily movement

- unpredictable bodily reactions

- the strangeness of “my body doing something without my intention”

These experiences create a framework in which farting is understood as a bodily outburst.

This embodied understanding positions laughter not as a simple stimulus response,
but as a phenomenon emerging at the intersection of body and self‑perception.

---

### 3.4 Layer Four: Cultural Meaning (Taboo)
In many cultures, farting is treated as “unclean.”
Children learn this through adult reactions and social norms:

- smell = impurity

- excretion = shame

- prohibition in public spaces

- adults’ contradictory responses (laughing vs. scolding)

This cultural taboo reinforces the idea that “a fart is a special event,”  
thereby amplifying the intensity of laughter.

---

### 3.5 Integration of the Four Layers
These layers do not operate independently.
They form a temporal and conceptual sequence:

sound (immediate) → odor (delayed) → bodily experience → cultural meaning

This integration explains why a fart becomes
not “just a sound,”
but a deep concept capable of eliciting laughter.

Through this layered structure, farting becomes a unique conceptual phenomenon
in which disgust and humor can coexist.
This point will be revisited in Chapter 7.

---

### 3.6 Comparison with Sneezing: Why Is the Laughter Weaker?
Although sneezing also produces a sudden sound, it lacks:

- odor

- strong impurity associations

- cultural taboo

- bodily experience of “outburst” (it is perceived more as a reflex)

As a result, the conceptual depth of sneezing is shallow,
and the intensity of laughter is correspondingly weak.

This contrast supports the study’s claim that odor and taboo  
play central roles in deepening the concept and strengthening laughter.

# 4. A Mathematical Model of Fart‑Induced Laughter (Formalization of This Study)

### 4.1 Why a Probabilistic Model?
This study treats children’s laughter toward farting as a binary outcome—
it either occurs or does not occur.
However, the emergence of laughter is not governed by a simple threshold.
It is a probabilistic phenomenon influenced cumulatively by
the immediate stimulus and the depth of the underlying concept.

For this reason, the probability of laughter is modeled using a logistic function:

$$
P_{\text{laugh}} = \sigma(Z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-Z}}
$$

The logistic model smoothly maps the linear combination $𝑍$   
of stimulus and conceptual factors into a probability between 0 and 1,
making it well‑suited for quantifying the “likelihood” of laughter.

---

### 4.2 Defining the Stimulus Level 𝑆  (Sound + Odor)

The stimulus level $𝑆$ of a fart is composed of the immediate strength of its sound
and the delayed arrival of its odor:

$$
S = a_{\text{sound}} \, S_{\text{sound}} + a_{\text{smell}} \, S_{\text{smell}}
$$

- $𝑆_{\text{sound}}$ : suddenness, loudness, and rhythmic irregularity of the sound

- $𝑆_{\text{smell}}$ : intensity of the odor (delayed stimulus)

- $𝑎_{\text{sound}},𝑎_{\text{smell}}$ : weights

This corresponds to the first two layers of the four‑layer model:

Layer 1: immediacy of sound

Layer 2: delayed odor

---

### 4.3 Defining the Concept Level 𝐶  (Accumulated Experience)
The maturity of the concept $𝐶$  is built from accumulated experiences of odor,
bodily sensations, and cultural learning:

$$
C = b_{\text{smell}} \, E_{\text{smell}} + b_{\text{body}} \, E_{\text{body}} + b_{\text{culture}} \, E_{\text{culture}}
$$

- $𝐸_{\text{smell}}$ : accumulated odor experiences (disgust, excretion associations)

- $𝐸_{\text{body}}$ : bodily experiences of expulsion (self–body linkage)

- $𝐸_{\text{culture}}$ : adult reactions and internalized taboos

- $𝑏_{\text{smell}},𝑏_{\text{body}},𝑏_{\text{culture}}$ : weights

This corresponds to:

Layer 2: odor memory

Layer 3: bodily experience

Layer 4: cultural meaning

---

### 4.4 Defining the Integrated Variable 𝑍  (Stimulus × Concept)
Stimulus $𝑆$ and concept $𝐶$ are integrated into an internal variable 
$𝑍$ that determines the probability of laughter:

$$
Z = w_s \, S + w_c \, C + b
$$

- $𝑤_{\text{𝑠}}$ : weight of stimulus

- $𝑤_{\text{𝑐}}$ : weight of conceptual depth

- $𝑏$ : bias term

A larger $𝑍$ corresponds to a higher probability of laughter.

 ---

## 6. A Formal Explanation of the Differences Between Farting and Sneezing
This chapter compares the structures of the fart model (Chapter 4) and the sneeze model (Chapter 5) to formally demonstrate why the probability of laughter differs systematically between the two.

In conclusion, this study shows:

- 𝐶_fart > 𝐶_sneeze

- 𝑍_fart > 𝑍_sneeze

- therefore 𝑃(laugh∣fart) > 𝑃(laugh∣sneeze)

### 6.1 Difference in Stimulus Level: Presence or Absence of Odor
The stimulus level for farting is:

$$
S_{\text{fart}} = a_{\text{sound}} \, S_{\text{sound}} + a_{\text{smell}} \, S_{\text{smell}}
$$

In contrast, the stimulus level for sneezing is:

$$
S_{\text{sneeze}} = a_{\text{sound-sneeze}} \, S_{\text{sound-sneeze}} + a_{\text{motion-sneeze}} \, S_{\text{motion}}
$$

The decisive difference is:

- Farts include an odor stimulus 𝑆_smell

- Sneezes do not include 𝑆smell

Odor strongly evokes meanings such as impurity, disgust, and excretion.
Therefore, 𝑆_fart tends to be larger than 𝑆_sneeze .

---

### 6.2 Difference in Concept Level: Odor Experience and Taboo
The conceptual level for farting is:

$$
C_{\text{fart}} = b_{\text{smell}} \, E_{\text{smell}} + b_{\text{body}} \, E_{\text{body}} + b_{\text{culture}} \, E_{\text{culture}}
$$

The conceptual level for sneezing is:

$$
C_{\text{sneeze}} = b_{\text{body-sneeze}} \, E_{\text{body-sneeze}} + b_{\text{culture-sneeze}} \, E_{\text{culture-sneeze}} 
$$

The structural differences are clear.

Present in farting
- odor experience 𝐸_smell
- strong impurity/taboo culture 𝐸_culture

Absent in sneezing
- odor experience 𝐸_smell
- strong taboo (sneezing is culturally associated with care, not impurity)

Thus, structurally:

$$
C_{\text{fart}} > C_{\text{sneeze}}
$$

---

### 6.3 Difference in Integrated Variable $𝑍$ : Cumulative Effects of Stimulus and Concept

The integrated variables are:

$$
Z_{\text{fart}} = w_{\text{s}} \, S_{\text{fart}} + w_{\text{c}} \, C_{\text{fart}} + b
$$

$$
Z_{\text{sneeze}} = w_{\text{s-sneeze}} \, S_{\text{sneeze}} + w_{\text{c-sneeze}} \, C_{\text{sneeze}} + b_{\text{sneeze}} 
$$

As shown in previous sections:

- 𝑆_fart > 𝑆_sneeze

- 𝐶_fart > 𝐶_sneeze

Therefore, as long as the weights are positive:

$$
Z_{\text{fart}} > Z_{\text{sneeze}}
$$

---

### 6.4 Resulting Difference in Probability
Since the probability of laughter is given by the logistic function:

$$
P(\text{laugh}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-Z}}
$$

a larger $𝑍$ yields a higher probability.

Thus:

$$
Z_{\text{fart}} > Z_{\text{sneeze}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad P(\text{laugh} \mid \text{fart}) > P(\text{laugh} \mid \text{sneeze})
$$


This aligns with everyday observation.

### 6.5 Summary: The Essence of the Difference
The differences shown in this chapter arise not merely from differences in stimulus, but from the presence or absence of elements that create conceptual depth:

- odor stimulus (𝑆_smell)

- odor experience (𝐸_smell)

- taboo culture (𝐸_culture)

In other words:

Farting forms a “deep concept,” whereas sneezing remains a “shallow concept.”

This structural difference manifests as a difference in the intensity of laughter.

## 7. Discussion
The four‑layer structural model and the two‑layer probabilistic model presented in this study offer a perspective in which children’s laughter toward farting is not a simple stimulus response, but a complex phenomenon emerging at the intersection of embodiment, cognition, and culture.

### 7.1 Re‑evaluating Children’s Embodied Knowledge
The findings of this study challenge the traditional view that children laugh at farts merely as a reflexive reaction to stimuli.

- 𝑆_smell : the immediate stimulus

This is a core point of novelty in the present study.

In many cultures, farting is framed as “unclean.”

This cultural layer functions as a conceptual amplifier,
increasing the intensity of laughter.

In contrast, sneezing is embedded in a culture of care (“Are you okay?”, “Bless you”),

### 7.5 Farting as Value Inversion: How Negative Value Becomes Laughter
Farting carries inherently “negative” values—odor, impurity, shame.

Thus, laughter at farting arises from a cognitive leap:

a transformation from negative value to positive value.

This value inversion cannot be explained by simple stimulus–response models.
It is a higher‑order cognitive phenomenon in which embodiment, concept formation,
and cultural meaning interact in layered and mutually reinforcing ways.
