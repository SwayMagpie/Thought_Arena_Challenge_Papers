# TACP #2 — VOID LINE
**A Thought Experiment on Value Generation Using Queue and Void**

## 1. Introduction: Where Does Value Come From?
Humans assign value even to things that have no predefined worth.
We expect, interpret, and evaluate.

Standing in a line makes us think “there must be something.”

Seeing others line up makes us feel “this must be valuable.”

Spending time makes us want to “justify the cost.”

These reactions sit at the intersection of behavioral economics, social psychology, and information theory—
the fundamental question of how value is generated.

This challenge asks the question:

“Can value emerge from nothing but a queue and a void?”

---

## 2. The Setup: The VOID LINE Apparatus
VOID LINE is an extremely minimal device composed of only three elements.

#### (1) The Queue
A staff member stands on a busy street corner holding a sign that says “End of the Line.”
No explanation.
No guidance.

#### (2) The Void
When you reach the front of the line, you find:

- a blank white space

- a simple chair

- a silent room

Nothing more.
A pure void.

#### (3) Pay-What-You-Want
At the exit, a box reads:
“Pay whatever you wish (0 acceptable).”  
The staff does not react.
There is zero pressure.

---

## 3. Psychological Processes Triggered by the Device
VOID LINE naturally induces several psychological responses:

- Social proof: others are lining up, so it must be valuable

- Value of uncertainty: the unknown generates expectation

- Sunk cost effect: you want to justify the time spent

- Self-justification: you try to assign meaning after seeing the void

- Pay-what-you-want tension: 0 feels like “I didn’t understand the value”

All of these suggest that value is generated internally, not externally.

---

## 4. Your Challenge
This is the core of the challenge.

You were dragged into the line by the person you’re with.
You didn’t want to argue, so you joined.

You wait for about 30 minutes.

People around you seem expectant.

You begin to think, “Maybe there’s something here.”

Finally, you reach the front.

There is nothing.

- A white room.
- A chair.
- Silence.

At the exit, the pay-what-you-want box awaits.
Your companion waits a short distance away.  
They can see whether you pay or not, but **not how much**.  
You reach for your wallet.


Question: How much do you put in?

- 1

- 5

- 10

- 20

- More

- Or walk away without paying

The number you choose quietly reflects your internal value system.
The subtle “disturbance” that arises in the process is precisely what this device seeks to observe.

---

## 5. The Aftermath: The Hell of Justification
You leave VOID LINE and sit at a nearby café.
Your companion sips their coffee, looks straight at you, and asks:

“So… how much did you put in? And why?”

There is no escape.
They are trying to see the bottom of your value system.

You must choose whether to:

- tell the truth

- hide it

- fabricate a reason

- defend your decision

- align with your companion’s values

### Question A: Do you reveal the real amount?

- Can you admit it if it was 0?

- Can you admit it if it was high?

- Do you lie to avoid judgment?

### Question B: Does your explanation hold together?
- “I didn’t want to waste my time.”

- “No particular reason.”

- “I felt embarrassed.”

- “It felt like it had some value.”

- “It’s just for the story.”

Your explanation may be nothing more than a post-hoc narrative.

### Question C: What if your companion’s answer contradicts yours?
- You: 0  → Companion: 20

- You: 15 → Companion: 0

In that moment, your intelligence, values, and judgment are shaken.

Do you:

- hold your ground

- deny their judgment

- revise your own values

- think “maybe I was wrong”

- or still find meaning in your choice?

The Aftermath is the second experiment of VOID LINE.
The line and the void test your internal value generation.
The Aftermath tests your social coherence.

- Is your value system consistent?

- Does it waver in front of others?

- Can you justify it?

- Are you influenced by someone else’s values?

VOID LINE ends in emptiness.
The Aftermath ends in the revelation of your true values.

## 6. Author’s Predictions (Hypotheses)
This section serves as the author’s stance—
not a “correct answer,” but a way to shape the spark.

#### Hypothesis 1: Queue length becomes a proxy for value
Longer lines → higher payments.

#### Hypothesis 2: Payment amounts form a wide distribution
- A stable percentage will pay 0

- The 1 - 5 range will be the thickest

- A few will pay surprisingly high amounts

#### Hypothesis 3: Companions increase payment amounts
Social comparison (“0 is embarrassing”) will influence decisions.

#### Hypothesis 4: Meaning-making after the void varies widely
- Some interpret it as contemporary art

- Some feel it was a waste of time

- Some treat it as a joke worth paying for

#### Hypothesis 5: Value is generated from the void
Even with zero predefined value, humans create meaning and generate value.
